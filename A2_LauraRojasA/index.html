Nearly 600 organics drop-off sites will lose the budget to operate in 2024

Budget cuts put forward by Major Eric Adams reduce funding for the New York Department of Sanitation, potentially eliminating community composting programs in charge of running drop-off composting sites for residents.

By: Laura Rojas Aponte | December 19, 2023

Currently, New Yorkers can find organic drop-off sites in all five boroughs. These are specific locations where residents can leave their food scraps and yard waste to reduce carbon emissions and produce soil fertilizer. Regardless of the neighborhood, the city offers about 400 sites that are free and open to anyone who wants to participate in the collection. However, the availability of these sites may soon change.

Recent budget cuts proposed by the Adams Administration eliminate funding for community composting programs and, consequently, defund the operation of organic drop-off sites. These cuts are part of a larger effort to reduce city spending. If approved, the administration’s plan could affect 591 drop-off locations serving communities at farmers' markets, community gardens, subway entrances, and even smart bins, according to open data offered by NYSD.

Around 400 of these sites are operated by NYDS, and 36% are run by third parties that tend to specialize in composting and get paid by the city to run the operations. One of them is the non-profit Grow NYC, which operates 46 locations.This particular organization has faced the situation by organizing sign-up petitions from residents and independent press conferences at City Hall. GrowNYC secured funding on their own, thanks to an anonymous donor. GrowNYC compost coordinator Erick Menjivar said he was relieved, 'It was great news, we won’t lose our jobs.'

These budget cuts came in the same year the city approved the legislative package Zero Waste Act, and, with it, ramped up composting efforts, such as the expansion of mandatory curbside composting in Brooklyn and Queens – which would also be affected.
